  (a) The goal, in my words

  Procivic turns a voter's own stated values into a personalized,
  evidence-backed verdict on every item of one real ballot — the June 2, 2026 SF
  (CA-11) consolidated primary. A ~60s quiz (≈10 ballot-tailored issues, each
  stance × importance) produces a value vector; the app then shows the whole 
  ballot, each item carrying a recommendation + confidence + a one-line why,
  every claim traceable to a cited source. Drilling in reveals the receipts —
  standardized stances, voting record, an interactive funding graph,
  consistency/transparency scores, and a grounded, citation-only "Ask Procivic"
  Q&A. The math is deterministic and reproducible (all weights in config/ + a
  methodology page); the AI only ever explains the 
  user's-values-vs-public-record computation and never editorializes; and all
  source data is precomputed + adversarially verified into static /data JSON so
  nothing hits a live API on stage. "Done" = every RUBRIC.md gate passes under a
  fresh verifier subagent — I never self-certify, and I gate each slice with
  the matching verifier-prompts.md prompt before moving on.

  (b) Slice-0 proposal — brand & design system

  gradients, tasteful glass. One critical guardrail given the domain: red is 
  purely a semantic "conflict / contradiction / alert" accent, never a party

  blue=D / red=R.

❯ How is this app currently significantly different than isidewith.com?         

⏺ Fair and important question — it's the one a judge will ask, and the brief
  explicitly positions against exactly this. Straight answer, including where
  the difference is real today vs. still thin.

  The core difference: evidence basis

  iSideWith matches you to candidates on what they say — their stated positions,
  run through a quiz with importance weighting. Procivic's whole thesis is that
  talk is the weakest signal. So for the deep case it derives a candidate's
  stances from:

  1. How they actually voted — Wiener's issue stances come from real CA State
  Senate roll-call votes (OpenStates), each one traceable to the specific bill.
  iSideWith has no concept of a voting record.
  2. Who funds them — an interactive funding graph from real OpenFEC filings
  (and SF-Ethics money for the measures' YES/NO sides). iSideWith doesn't touch
  money at all.
  3. Consistency — "said X, voted Y" — it compares stated positions against the
  votes and surfaces contradictions with the donor who benefited. This is
  structurally impossible for a stated-positions-only tool. (This is the piece
  being built right now, and it only shows a "receipt" if a real documented gap
  exists — no manufactured drama.)

  Other genuine differentiators

  - Confidence + citations, not a black box. Every recommendation carries a
  confidence score (driven by how much real data backs your priorities), every
  claim links to its primary source, and a /methodology page renders every 
  weight — nothing hidden. iSideWith's match % is proprietary and uncited.
  - Whole real ballot, including local measures. It decodes one specific ballot
  end-to-end — crucially the 4 SF props with plain-language summaries +
  for/against funding. Local measures are where voters are most lost and where
  iSideWith is weakest/absent.
  - Grounded, cited Q&A that refuses rather than guesses when the record is
  silent.
  - Honest data-tiering. It labels what it doesn't know (Tier-3 "stated
  positions only"). iSideWith presents everything with equal confidence.

  Where I'd be honest with you

  - The quiz → weighted-alignment mechanic itself is not novel — iSideWith did
  stance × importance years ago. Our elicitation side overlaps.
  - The votes/money/consistency depth is currently realized for Tier-1 (Wiener) 
  only. For the other candidates we fall back to stated positions — which is
  precisely the iSideWith approach. So for non-incumbents we're not yet
  differentiated on evidence; we're just more honest about flagging it.
  - Breadth is iSideWith's moat (every race, nationally). Ours is depth + 
  auditability on one ballot.

  One-line version: iSideWith tells you who agrees with what you say; Procivic
  shows you, with receipts, whether the record — votes and money — actually
  backs that up, and how confident it is.

  If we want that gap to land in the demo, the move is to lean the 60-second
  path hard into the Wiener vote-derived stances + funding graph + (if real) the
  consistency receipt — the three things iSideWith fundamentally can't show.
  The two agents finishing now (measure verification + the
  consistency/transparency data) are exactly that differentiating layer. Want me
  to keep going, or reshape priorities given this framing?

  What a 50% alignment score means

  The scale runs 0–100, measuring how closely your stances match the item's on
  the issues you said you care about (weighted by your importance):
  - Per issue: agree = 1 − |your_position − their_position| / 2 — identical = 1,
  exact opposite = 0, and one being neutral (0) while the other is strong (±1) 
  = 0.5.
  - Overall = the importance-weighted average of those, ×100.

  So 50% is the midpoint — "neither aligned nor opposed; net neutral / mixed."
  It happens in a few ways:
  1. You're neutral on the issues where they take strong positions (each
  contributes ~0.5), or
  2. Your agreements and disagreements cancel out (you match them on some
  priorities, clash on others), or
  3. There's nothing to compare — the item has no stance on any issue you
  weighted (or you haven't answered the quiz). In that case the engine returns a
  neutral 50 as a fallback, not a real measurement.

  ▎ editorializes — it computes your values vs. the public record, every claim

  ▎ when the record is silent, and falls back to deterministic cited output if
